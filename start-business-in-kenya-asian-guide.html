How Asians Can Start a Business in Kenya – Step-by-Step Guide (2025)

From registration to market strategy — Nexfield Africa shares what every Asian entrepreneur needs to know about starting up in Kenya.

Nexfield Africa Business Consulting Team
June 16, 2025 7 min read

Why Kenya?

Kenya is the regional business hub of East Africa — with growing digital infrastructure, trade agreements like AfCFTA, and a vibrant consumer market. This makes it a magnet for Asian investors ready to scale across borders.

Step-by-Step: How to Set Up Your Business

  1. Reserve a company name via eCitizen
  2. Register with BRS (Business Registration Service)
  3. Apply for KRA PIN and open a Kenyan bank account
  4. Apply for a Class G Work Permit (for investors)
  5. Secure local business licenses based on your sector

Top Sectors for Asian Founders

  • Import/export and logistics consulting
  • E-commerce & fulfillment hubs
  • Electronics, beauty & specialty retail
  • Restaurants & Asian cuisine franchises

Cultural and Market Tips

Kenyans value relationships and reputation. Building trust locally — especially through partnerships, fair pricing, and community engagement — goes a long way.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Asians legally start a business in Kenya?

Yes, foreigners including Asians can legally start businesses in Kenya. You’ll need to follow the registration process through the eCitizen portal and obtain the necessary permits and tax registrations.

What’s the minimum capital requirement for foreigners?

In most cases, foreign investors are required to show proof of capital investment, often USD 100,000 or more, depending on the sector and ownership structure.

What sectors are most suitable for Asian entrepreneurs?

Popular sectors include import/export, retail, e-commerce, food, real estate, and technology. Logistics and manufacturing are also growing areas.

Do I need a local partner to register a business?

No, but it’s highly recommended for ease of operation, compliance, and market access. Some industries may require local shareholding.
